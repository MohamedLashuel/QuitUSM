# About the book

# Introduction

## What makes social media addictive
- Intentional design
- Dopamine
-- Reward for finding information/entertainment
-- Reward for (fake) social connection
-- Behavioral reinforcement, downregulation
-- Variable reward scheme: scrolling and likes
- FOMO
- Quick availability
- Down time (filling voids)

## Consequences (benefits of quitting)
- Time
- Procrastination
- Distraction/concentration ability
- Tiredness
- Attention span
- Withdrawal
- Sleep
- Negative self image
- Anxiety
- Shame
- Awareness
- Decreased emotional awareness, less motivation (HG: end-stage screen addiction)

# What is addiction
- A split between wanting to use and wanting to quit

## Don't addicts not have control? (Free will)
- Experiments on alcohol/cocaine
- Brain scan misconception
- Disease model history
- Believing this keeps you trapped (addiction brainwashing, adderall/painkillers)
- See Appendix A

## Who would want to be an addict?
- Addicts don't choose to be addicts, they choose to use
-- They wish they had never started
- Using is seen as the happier option - quitting would make them miserable

# Why quitting is difficult

## The willpower method
- (Right from slide)

## Cutting down
- Moderate use is not easier
- The nature of any addiction is to want more
- Constantly keeps the addiction alive
- Social media usage becomes "precious"

## Fear of quitting
- Fear of quitting comes from fear of cravings, fear of boredom, FOMO
- We know that social media is bad for us but see quitting as worse
- Reframe costs of quitting to benefits of quitting
- Fear is irrational, we know what it's like to not use

## Negative emotions from quitting
- Shame/sadness/etc. are brought up when not using and suppressed by using
- Back to front again: Using screens makes it go away

# Quitting
- Decide what to cut and cut it
- Have a positive attitude: rejoice
- Use a site blocker (adding friction) and using screens less
- Replacement

## Withdrawals/cravings
- Withdrawals are minor physically, mostly mental
- Cravings are something you do
- Dealing with habitual thoughts

## Relapse
- No big deal unless it's perceived as such. Resilience is necessary

## Other tips
- Disable notifications
- Black and white
- Designate a no-phone area
